NASA/ADS
Modeling T Tauri Winds from He I λ10830 Profiles
The high opacity of He I λ10830 makes it an exceptionally sensitive probe of the inner wind geometry of accreting T Tauri stars. In this line, blueshifted absorption below the continuum results from simple scattering of stellar photons, a situation that is readily modeled without definite knowledge of the physical conditions and recourse to multilevel radiative transfer. We present theoretical line profiles for scattering in two possible wind geometries, a disk wind and a wind emerging radially from the star, and compare them to observed He I λ10830 profiles from a survey of classical T Tauri stars. The comparison indicates that subcontinuum blueshifted absorption is characteristic of disk winds in ~30% of the stars and of stellar winds in ~40%. We further conclude that for many stars the emission profile of helium likely arises in stellar winds, increasing the fraction of accreting stars inferred to have accretion-powered stellar winds to ~60%. Stars with the highest disk accretion rates are more likely to have stellar wind than disk wind signatures and less likely to have redshifted absorption from magnetospheric funnel flows. This suggests the possibility that when accretion rates are high, disks can extend closer to the star, magnetospheric accretion zones can be reduced in size, and conditions can arise that favor radially outflowing stellar winds.
Original URL: https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2007ApJ...657..897K/abstract

I all, I have searched the litterature to understand the origin of the Fe emission lines, and I have found this one: https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/1979ApJ...234..949C/abstract

NASA/ADS
Atmospheres of T Tau stars : the photosphere and low chromosphere.
A model for the photospheric and low chromospheric regions of the atmosphere of an advanced T Tauri star has been constructed, by superposing an ad hoc temperature rise on a theoretical model photosphere. This model has been used to synthesize several spectral features, including the continuum from 300 A to 300 microns and the Fe I and Fe II spectrum in the interval 4475-4500 A. A comparison between these predictions and observations supports the conclusion that T Tauri stars possess a chromosphere that begins deep in the atmosphere, with the beginning depth of the chromosphere principally determining the degree of advancement of the T Tauri spectrum. There are a number of spectral features that cannot be explained by this model, including the near-IR excess, the large H alpha:H beta decrement, and the asymmetry of emission lines.
Original URL: https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/1979ApJ...234..949C/abstract
Evelyne (evelyne.alecian@univ-grenoble-alpes.fr)

Very interesting ! Here is part of the conclusion they give: “The results of the model building and spectral synthesis reported here provide very strong support for Herbig’s suggestion that many spectral features in T Tauri stars can be explained in terms of a deep chromosphere. We have shown in semiquantitative terms that the features which can be explained in terms of a simple temperature model and the condition of hydrostatic equilibrium include: (i) the absence of absorption lines in advanced T Tauri stars and their weakness and diffuseness in the less advanced stars; (ii) the existence of a “blue continuum” produced by the filling in of some photospheric lines and the presence of the stronger lines in emission ; (iii) the Balmer continuum excess; (iv) the existence of a strong EUV continuum ; (v) the general shapes of the flux profiles of Ca n H and K and Na Dl5  in particular the existence of a narrow emission core bordered by broad absorption wings; and (vi) the widths of the Balmer lines. The features that have not been explained include: (i) the near-IR excess; (ii) the large Ha:Hß decrement; (iii) the asymmetries and displaced absorption components seen in many lines; and (iv) the intensity ratios of the Ca n IR triplet.”

*Thread Reply:* the FAP is the probability of measuring a peak in the periodogram at a given height. Indeed it allows to remove the periods due to gaussian noise, a FAP of 0.01 means that there is only 1% chance to obtain a significant peak here without periodic signal, the lower is the FAP, the better it is. We can easily reach the 10^-2 to 10^-3, and up to 10^-4 in case of strong periodic signal (such as IPC on HQ Tau as example)

The global shape of the Halpha profile has not changed much, except in narrow parts in the blue and red wings, which we may attribute to wind and accretion signatures respectively. Also, while in 2011 the auto-correlation matrix shows several substructures, in 2021 the profile of Halpha appears to be constituted of only two major correlated substructures (one from -400 to 250, and one from 300 to 400 km/s). The periodogram of 2021 does not show any clear periodicity, except maybe between -450 and -400 km/s, but the signal is so faint in the Halpha profile there, that I am not sure that it is realistic. It is interesting to not that the magnetic Zeeman signatures are much stronger in 2011 than in 2021, and that the accretion signature is much more evident in 2011 than in 2021.

A poster about RU Lup in Dublin’s meeting: they derive veiling from the Li I line. https://zenodo.org/record/5566878#.YW2YjS2B2Rs

Zenodo
Accretion Activity in RU Lup
Studying the process of accretion and its variability is vital in understanding a star's evolution and provides insight into the complex processes at work between the inner disk and accretion onto the stellar surface. An important quantity needed to do this is the accretion luminosity ($L_{acc}$). A common problem in obtaining values for $L_{acc}$ and the associated mass accretion rate ($\dot{M}_{acc}$) in young stars is the lack of flux calibrated data. Many archival observations exist of classical T-Tauri stars (CTTS) and similar young stars, however, an absolute measurement of the flux has always been necessary to calculate these quantities. Here we have used 12 (almost) consecutive nights of optical spectroscopy obtained from the spectropolarimeter ESPaDOnS of CTTS RU\,Lup to examine the relationship between the $L_{acc}$ and continuum veiling of the star. We estimate the continuum veiling from the \ion{Li}{i} 670.7 nm line, a process that does not require flux calibrated data. RU\,Lup is a strong accretor and therefore has very strong veiling, dominated by line emission, however it is possible to isolate the continuum veiling using the \ion{Li}{i} line. Our result is an empirical relation between the $L_{acc}$ (estimated using flux calibrated spectra) and the continuum veiling. The nature of this linear fit has the potential to be of great use to the study of CTTS and their accretion activity.
Original URL: https://zenodo.org/record/5566878#.YW2YjS2B2Rs
