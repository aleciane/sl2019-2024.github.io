MIAPbP program “Stellar Magnetic Fields from Protostars to Supernovae”
It will be held at MIAPbP (Munich Institute for Astro-, Particle and Bio-Physics) from 9 October to 3 November 2023. This is a 4-week residential workshop aimed at advancing our understanding of stellar magnetism through long-term focused research and discussion amongst experts and young scientists in the field. MIAPbP requests a minimum attendance of 10 working days, and each selected participant can claim up to 80 euros per day to cover their expenses (see https://www.munich-iapbp.de/for-visitors/financial-support). Postdocs and advanced PhD students are strongly welcome to apply. PhD students may also apply for a stipend covering up to 500 Euro of their travel expenses (in addition to the 80 euros per day).

Hello everyone,

I’ll be defending my PhD on 30/09/2024 at 2 pm, Paris time. If you happen to be in Paris on that day you are welcome to join us at CEA to assist. If not, a zoom link is provided below.

Title: How are stars born? The second gravitational collapse and its consequences Abstract: Stars form as a result of the collapse of gravitationally unstable molecular cloud cores. This process, despite having occurred countless times in the Universe, remains poorly understood because of the challenges involved in observing star forming regions, as well the theoretical difficulties associated with modelling a highly non-linear process in which self-gravitating hydrodynamics, magnetic fields, radiative transfer, and turbulence, all play out simultaneously and exhibit a complex web of interactions. In this regard, numerical simulations have offered invaluable insights that have pioneered much of our understanding in the field. Most notably, these have revealed a two-step evolutionary sequence in which a first core in hydrostatic equilibrium forms, which itself collapses to form a protostar. This second gravitational collapse is a highly dynamical process, and as such, is a challenging undertaking from a numerical point of view. During my thesis, I have carried out state-of-the-art radiative resistive-magnetohydrodynamics simulations of the collapse of turbulent dense cores to stellar densities. The dynamical range involving 25 orders of magnitude in density and 8 in spatial extent is tackled with the adaptive mesh refinement code RAMSES. The properties of the nascent protostar and that of its circumstellar disk are studied in unprecedented detail, and the calculations are pushed beyond many previous papers in the literature in my attempt to model the early main accretion phase. The results of these simulations have offered valuable insights on some of star formation’s outstanding issues. Firstly, I carried out radiative-hydrodynamics calculations in spherical symmetry with the goal of studying the properties and behavior of the nascent protostar, where I found that it is turbulent at birth despite its stability against convective motion. This is due to an instability at the accretion shock that is triggered shortly following protostellar birth. The turbulence is then sustained by accretion. This has implications for pre-stellar evolutionary models, as well as on the evolution of the magnetic field implanted in the protostar at birth. Following that, I have carried out simulations with turbulence in the initial dense core, which lead to the formation of circumstellar disks surrounding the protostar. The properties of the nascent disk, as well as its evolution over time, are studied in detail. Remarkably, we find that a circumstellar disk is formed following the rotational breakup of the protostar as the latter rapidly accretes materials from its surroundings. Finally, I have carried out simulations accounting for magnetic fields, which drastically alter the gas dynamics through magnetic braking. We find that a magnetic field of kilogauss strength is implanted in the protostar at birth, and despite magnetic braking, it nevertheless reaches breakup velocity and forms a circumstellar disk. The magnetization of the disk causes efficient angular momentum transport, which leads to different disk properties than in the hydrodynamical runs. These state-of-the-art results have shed light on the smallest spatial scales relevant to protostellar and circumstellar disk formation and evolution, thus furthering our understanding of the objects that later become fully-fledged planetary systems.

Hello everyone, I have some questions that are meant to bounce back on our discussions of last thursday afternoon.

  1. During said discussions, it was mentioned by @Oleg Kochukhov that the predominance of small scale magnetic fields in class I sources precludes the possibility of a static maintenance of the magnetic field after its implantation. I understand that one might expect such small scale fields to be diffused by turbulent motion within the protostar, but is there a possibility that it is not the case? In my simulations, a small scale magnetic field is implanted in the protostar at birth, and for the first half-year (I know it’s short), it seems to be maintained despite the turbulence. I might be resolving some sort of dynamo mechanism, as my Bz component is increasing in the protostar at the expense of Bphi , but how can we say for certain that static maintenance of a small scale field is out of the question?
  2. It was also mentioned that the sources observed by Johns-Krull, Yang & Johns-Krull, as well as C. Flores are in the saturation regime should they have undertaken any dynamo process. How certain are we of this?
Evelyne (evelyne.alecian@univ-grenoble-alpes.fr)
2024-11-22 17:31:44

Hi Adnan. To answer your first question, I would first suggest you to get an estimate of the diffusion timescale of small scale magnetic fields in a turbulent protostar. My expectation is that it will be quite fast, compared to the star formation process (i.e. at the scale of 10^5-6 years). Secondly, observations of more evolved stars show how the large-scale magnetic field can change over months-years timescales, most likely due to dynamo processes. It would be hard to believe that the large-scale B-field changes, but not the small scale.

Adnan Ali Ahmad (adnan.ali.ahmad1998@gmail.com)
2024-11-25 10:10:16

Regarding 1: I’m more convinced by your second argument than the first one. The ohmic diffusion timescale is of the order of a few billion years (Cowling 1945), and the turbulent diffusion timescale isn’t really known. Chabrier & Kuker 2006 estimated it to be 10-100 years, which is indeed short, but I’ve no idea how they estimated the turbulent diffusivity. Durney+ 1993 argues for the generation of a small scale magnetic field through a dynamo process, and I’m currently reading that paper to learn more. Your second argument sounds more promising; if a dynamo mechanism is generating/maintaining the large scale field, one might expect it to generate some small scale components as well. Regarding 2: Both I guess? On what are we basing our argument that they’ve reached the saturation regime?
